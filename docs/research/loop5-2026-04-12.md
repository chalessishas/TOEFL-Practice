# Research Loop 5 — 2026-04-12

## Q1: TOEFL 2026 Rubric Updates

### What changed (effective January 21, 2026)

The entire writing section was restructured. The old **Integrated Writing** (20 min, read+listen+write synthesis) and **Independent Essay** (30 min, 300+ word argumentative essay) are both **gone**. The new section has three tasks:

| Task | Time | Format | Scoring |
|------|------|--------|---------|
| Build a Sentence | ~6 min | 4-6 scrambled word items | Binary: correct/incorrect |
| Write an Email | ~7 min | Accomplish 3 communicative goals, 80-120 words | 0-5 rubric |
| Academic Discussion | ~10 min | Respond to professor prompt + 2 peer posts, min 100 words | 0-5 rubric |

**Total writing time: ~23 min** (down from 50 min). Section score reports on a **1-6 CEFR-aligned band** (not the old 0-30 scale).

### Academic Discussion rubric dimensions (0-5)

Four dimensions, in priority order:
1. **Purposeful communication** — clear position, explicit opinion
2. **Discussion contribution** — engagement with classmates' views; original input
3. **Language accuracy** — sentence variety, grammatical range
4. **Mechanics** — spelling, punctuation

Score 5 requires: relevant + well-elaborated explanations/examples, consistent language facility, **almost no** lexical/grammatical errors.  
Score 4: adequately elaborated, **few** errors.  
Score 3: mostly relevant, some noticeable errors that partially impede.

**Critical rubric change from old format**: old Score 5 allowed "occasional language errors"; new Score 5 requires "almost no" errors. Grammar weight has increased.

### What this means for our scorer

- **No word count scoring in rubric** — 100 words is a floor, not a scoring criterion. Quality > length.
- **Peer engagement is a scored dimension** (not a bonus) — our `taskSpecific` logic in organization.js already handles this, which is correct.
- **Grammar now more discriminating at top end** — "almost no errors" for 5 vs "few errors" for 4 is a tighter threshold than old format. Our grammar.js 7% weight may be slightly low for discussion task specifically.
- **Responses scored "as first drafts"** — minor errors that don't interfere with meaning won't prevent high score.
- **Acknowledge-then-counter** pattern for peer disagreement is specifically highlighted as effective for top scores. Our `engagementVerb` detection covers "I agree/disagree with" but misses explicit counter-argument patterns like "While [Name] makes a valid point, I would argue...".

## Q2: Uncountable Noun Denylist for Quantifier+Noun Pattern

### Background

Chinese L1 writers omit plural -s because Mandarin does not morphologically mark plurality. The proposed pattern flags `many/several/few + BARE_NOUN` (e.g., "many student") but must skip inherently uncountable nouns (e.g., "many research" → correct error, "much research" is standard).

**Root cause confirmed**: Chinese EFL learners show misconceptions of noun countability as the primary source of article and quantifier errors (ScienceDirect, 2019).

### Proposed denylist — TOEFL-relevant uncountables

These are nouns that appear commonly in TOEFL Academic Discussion prompts and where `many X` is always wrong:

```js
// Uncountable nouns — never take plural -s in standard academic English
// "many research" → flag; "many researches" → flag; "much research" → correct
const UNCOUNTABLE_DENYLIST = new Set([
  // Academic/argument nouns (highest TOEFL frequency)
  'research','evidence','information','knowledge','advice','feedback',
  'progress','understanding','education','learning','training',
  'literature','data','analysis','work','employment','unemployment',
  // Social/institutional
  'behavior','behaviour','culture','health','welfare','safety','justice',
  'violence','poverty','inequality','discrimination','corruption',
  'traffic','transportation','infrastructure','management',
  // Abstract qualities
  'happiness','freedom','creativity','intelligence','wisdom','courage',
  'confidence','motivation','determination','patience','trust',
  // Material/environmental
  'energy','pollution','sustainability','technology','software',
  'equipment','furniture','accommodation','news','weather',
])
```

### Detection pattern

Flag: `/\b(many|several|few|a number of)\s+([a-z]+)\b/gi` where the captured noun (lemmatized) is NOT in `UNCOUNTABLE_DENYLIST`.

Example: `"many student"` → flag (student not in denylist). `"many research"` → skip (research in denylist). `"several evidence"` → flag as different error ("evidence" is uncountable, so "several" is wrong — but this is a harder case).

### Dual-error detection for "quantifier + uncountable"

If noun IS in `UNCOUNTABLE_DENYLIST` AND quantifier is `many/several/few`, flag as **quantifier mismatch** (should be "much/little/a great deal of"):
- "many research" → "Use 'much research' or 'a great deal of research' — 'research' is uncountable"
- "several evidence" → "Use 'much evidence' or 'evidence' alone — 'evidence' is uncountable"

### Edge cases to handle

- `data` is grammatically plural in formal academic English but treated as uncountable by most ESL learners and most style guides now accept singular treatment — **include in denylist to avoid over-flagging**.
- `work` can be countable ("a work of art", "the works of Shakespeare") — denylist inclusion means we skip "many work" which is correctly flagged. **Acceptable false negative**.
- Corpus ambiguous: `technology`, `culture` can be countable in some contexts. Use denylist conservatively — include only where uncountable is dominant usage.

## Q3: Organization Discourse Markers — Gap Analysis

### Current implementation

`organization.js` has 6 categories with ~65 total markers:
- contrast (15), addition (8), conclusion (12), exemplification (10), sequence (12), elaboration (7)

Plus separate INFERENTIAL_MARKERS (7), CONCESSIVE_MARKERS (13), ADDITIVE_MARKERS (7) for ratio scoring.

### Research findings on missing markers

**From ETS e-rater research (Burstein et al.)**: e-rater identifies these functional sub-categories beyond our 6:
- **Belief/speculation markers**: `perhaps`, `possibly`, `arguably`, `presumably`, `it could be argued` — these signal academic hedging which proficient writers use
- **Attribution/source markers**: `according to`, `as noted by`, `studies show`, `research indicates` — distinct from elaboration
- **Comparison markers**: `similarly`, `likewise`, `in the same way`, `by comparison` — currently `similarly` is in `addition`, but the others are absent

**From corpus study on Jordanian EFL (PMC 2024)**: Advanced markers absent from both lower-proficiency writers and our current lists:
- Concessive advanced: `granted that`, `admittedly`, `to be sure`, `it is true that...but`
- Inferential advanced: `it follows that`, `in light of this`, `given that`
- Contrastive advanced: `by contrast` (we have "in contrast" but not "by contrast")

**From IELTS/TOEFL Academic Discussion guidance**: Peer-engagement discourse markers not in any current category:
- `building on [Name]'s point`, `to expand on what [Name] said`, `I would add to [Name]'s argument`

### Recommended additions

Add a 7th category — **hedging** — which ETS e-rater explicitly scores:

```js
hedging: ['perhaps','possibly','arguably','presumably','it could be argued',
          'it seems','it appears','one might argue','it is worth noting',
          'this suggests','this may indicate']
```

Add to **elaboration** (currently 7 markers, weakest category):
- `granted`, `admittedly`, `to be sure`, `it is true that`, `in light of`

Add to **contrast**:
- `by contrast`, `that said`, `at the same time` (currently in elaboration — move to contrast as dual-category)

Add to **addition**:
- `likewise`, `in the same way`, `by the same token`

Add to **exemplification**:
- `as an example`, `a case in point`, `this is evident in`

### Key finding from research

Two empirical findings directly affect our scoring:
1. **Presence vs. diversity**: Cohesion marker *frequency* alone does NOT distinguish score levels (ScienceDirect 2016). This validates our existing diversity-over-density approach.
2. **Inferential > additive** at higher levels: Proficient writers use `therefore/consequently/hence` more than `also/moreover/furthermore`. Our `ratioBonus` mechanism is correctly calibrated for this.

The main gap is **hedging markers** — a whole functional category used by academic writers to signal epistemic stance, which e-rater explicitly tracks but we have zero coverage of.

## Q4: Sentence Length Variance Scoring

### Current implementation

```js
// style.js, line 54-55
// stddev 0→0.2, 3→0.5, 6→0.8, 8+→1.0
varianceScore = Math.min(1, 0.2 + stddev / 10)
```

This is a linear function: `score = 0.2 + stddev/10`, capped at 1.0, floored at 0.2.

### What research found

**No published standard**: No research paper provides specific stddev thresholds for "good" ESL writing variance. The AES literature treats sentence length variance as one shallow feature among many, not a stand-alone scored dimension.

**Published examples** (analyzemywriting.com):
- Simple children's story passage: stddev = 2 words
- Oscar Wilde's "The Happy Prince": stddev = 10.98 words
- George Washington's 1796 address: stddev = 20.11 words

**ProWritingAid guidance**: Higher stddev = better variety; below ~3 stddev = monotone. No upper ceiling issue mentioned.

**AES research finding** (Nature Scientific Reports 2025): Modern AES systems have moved away from sentence length variance as a primary feature, noting it "neglects crucial factors like text structure and semantics." It's used only as a supplementary shallow feature alongside readability indices (Gunning-Fog, Flesch).

### Assessment of current calibration

Our formula `0.2 + stddev/10` maps:
- stddev=0 → 0.2 (minimum, which is generous — a monotone essay gets 0.2 not 0.0)
- stddev=3 → 0.5 (passes the children's story test — reasonable)
- stddev=8 → 1.0 (needs ~8-word stddev for max score)

For TOEFL Academic Discussion at ~120-130 words, a response typically has 6-9 sentences. With short essays, stddev naturally has high variance from sampling noise. An essay with 7 sentences of lengths [15, 12, 18, 8, 22, 14, 19] has mean=15.4, stddev=4.6 → varianceScore = 0.66.

**Assessment**: The current formula appears reasonably calibrated for short TOEFL essays. The 0.2 floor is appropriate since we're scoring first-draft students. The main concern is that the formula is unbounded — a stddev of 40 (implausible in practice but possible with one very long sentence) → score of 1.0 which is correct behavior.

**One calibration issue**: For Academic Discussion (6-10 sentences), a **coefficient of variation (stddev/mean)** might be more appropriate than raw stddev, because it normalizes for different mean sentence lengths. A writer using consistently long sentences (mean=25) with stddev=4 has more real variety than a writer using short sentences (mean=8) with stddev=4. Current formula treats them equally.

**Recommended change**: Consider `varianceScore = min(1, 0.2 + (stddev/mean) * 1.2)` which uses coefficient of variation. At mean=15, stddev=4 → CV=0.267 → score=0.52 (same as current roughly). At mean=8, stddev=4 → CV=0.5 → score=0.8 (inflated — short monotone essay gets rewarded for tiny absolute variation). This may not be better. **Conclusion: keep current formula**, it's defensible and changing it requires A/B test data we don't have.
